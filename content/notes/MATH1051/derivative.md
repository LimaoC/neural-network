The *derivative* of a [function](notes/MATH1051/functions.md) $f$ at a point $x$ is defined by

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h},$$

and $f$ is said to be differentiable at $x$ if [this limit exists](notes/MATH1051/limits.md). A function is said to be differentiable on an interval $[a, b]$ if it is differentiable at every point in $[a, b]$.

Geometrically, the derivative of a function is the gradient of the line tangent to the graph $f$ at the point $x$.

A differentiable function is [continuous](notes/MATH1051/continuity.md), but the converse is not necessarily true.
